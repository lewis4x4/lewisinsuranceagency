# Florida Uninsured Motorist Coverage: Essential Protection for 2026

With one in five Florida drivers lacking proper insurance coverage, uninsured motorist protection has never been more critical. As Florida prepares for major auto insurance law changes in 2026, understanding this vital coverage could mean the difference between financial protection and devastating out-of-pocket expenses after an accident.

## The Growing Problem of Uninsured Drivers in Florida

Florida faces a concerning reality: one in five drivers in Florida is uninsured, according to the latest 2025 study by the Insurance Research Council. This represents a significant increase from previous years, with Florida drivers revving up the trend more than the average, jumping from an estimated 16% of Florida drivers uninsured in 2022 to 20% in 2023.

Only five states and the District of Columbia have higher rates: Mississippi has the highest proportion, followed by New Mexico, D.C., Michigan, Tennessee and Missouri. For North Florida residents, including those in the Lake City area, this statistic means encountering an uninsured driver isn't just possible—it's probable.

### Why Are So Many Florida Drivers Uninsured?

Several factors contribute to Florida's high uninsured motorist rate:

- **Rising insurance costs**: The average yearly cost of auto insurance has increased from $789.29 in 2010 to $1,127 in 2022
- **Low minimum requirements**: Florida requires only $10,000 in personal injury protection and $10,000 for property damage
- **Economic pressures**: Many drivers simply cannot afford even basic coverage
- **Growing population**: New residents may be unaware of requirements or lack proper documentation

## What Is Uninsured Motorist Coverage?

Uninsured motorist coverage is a vital component of your auto insurance policy that protects you in the event of an accident involving an uninsured or underinsured driver. This type of coverage is designed to fill the financial gap left by the at-fault driver's lack of or insufficient insurance.

Uninsured motorist coverage comes in two main types:

### Uninsured Motorist Bodily Injury (UMBI)
UMBI coverage compensates for damages related to physical injuries, such as medical expenses, lost wages, future loss of earning potential, pain and suffering, and disability costs. It also covers wrongful death claims if the accident results in a fatality.

### Uninsured Motorist Property Damage (UMPD)
UMPD coverage covers damages to your vehicle and other property. This is particularly important in cases where the at-fault driver does not have property damage liability (PDL) insurance or has insufficient coverage to repair or replace your vehicle.

## Major Changes Coming in 2026

Florida's auto insurance landscape is undergoing its biggest transformation in over 50 years. Starting July 1, 2026, Florida may eliminate its PIP requirement entirely. After more than 50 years of no-fault insurance, the state could end personal injury protection coverage.

### How These Changes Affect Uninsured Motorist Coverage

The elimination of PIP makes uninsured motorist coverage even more critical. 15.9% of Florida drivers lack sufficient coverage, making uninsured motorist protection essential under the new system, especially with nearly one in six Florida drivers lacking adequate coverage.

Beginning July 1, 2026, the bill also makes uninsured motorist coverage insurers liable for non-economic damages, including pain and suffering, disability or physical impairment, disfigurement, mental anguish, inconvenience, and the loss of capacity for the enjoyment of life.

## Why Florida Drivers Need Uninsured Motorist Coverage

### Financial Protection Beyond PIP
Florida's minimum insurance comprises only $10,000 in personal injury protection (PIP) and $10,000 in property damage liability (PDL), amounts often insufficient to cover the full extent of damage. The lack of adequate insurance from the other party can lead to considerable out-of-pocket expenses for medical treatments, rehabilitation, and related costs.

### Hit-and-Run Protection
Uninsured motorist coverage also extends to scenarios beyond traditional car accidents, such as hit-and-run incidents. In North Florida's rural areas around Lake City, hit-and-run accidents on highways like I-75 and US-441 are unfortunately common.

### Cost-Effective Protection
The cost of uninsured motorist coverage is between $3 and $6 per month for a policy that covers injuries up to $25,000 per person and up to $50,000 per accident. In the current climate, that will add about 13% to the total, typical bill, according to Coral Springs insurance agent Andy Kasten.

## Understanding Florida's Current Requirements

Uninsured motorist coverage (UM coverage) is not mandatory under Florida law. This means drivers can opt out of uninsured motorist coverage by signing a waiver, but many insurance professionals and attorneys strongly recommend keeping it in place.

If you don't want uninsured motorist coverage, you must sign a waiver to decline it. However, given the statistics, declining this coverage puts you at significant financial risk.

## Stacked vs. Non-Stacked Coverage

Florida offers both stacked and non-stacked uninsured motorist coverage options:

- **Stacked coverage**: Allows you to combine coverage limits across multiple vehicles on your policy
- **Non-stacked coverage**: Limits coverage to the amount purchased for the specific vehicle involved in the accident

For families with multiple vehicles, stacked coverage often provides better protection at a reasonable cost increase.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is uninsured motorist coverage required in Florida?**
A: No, uninsured motorist coverage is optional in Florida, but you must sign a waiver to decline it. Given that one in five Florida drivers is uninsured, most insurance professionals strongly recommend keeping this coverage.

**Q: How much does uninsured motorist coverage cost?**
A: The cost is typically between $3 and $6 per month for coverage up to $25,000 per person and $50,000 per accident, making it an affordable way to protect against significant financial loss.

**Q: Will the 2026 law changes make uninsured motorist coverage mandatory?**
A: The proposed legislation does not make uninsured motorist coverage mandatory, but it significantly expands what these policies must cover, including non-economic damages like pain and suffering.

**Q: Should I choose stacked or non-stacked coverage?**
A: If you have multiple vehicles, stacked coverage often provides better protection by allowing you to combine coverage limits. Your Lewis Insurance Agency agent can help you determine which option best fits your situation.

**Q: What happens if I'm hit by an uninsured driver and don't have UM coverage?**
A: Without uninsured motorist coverage, you'll be responsible for your own medical bills, lost wages, and vehicle repairs beyond what your PIP or health insurance covers. You could also pursue a lawsuit against the uninsured driver, but collecting damages can be difficult if they lack assets.
