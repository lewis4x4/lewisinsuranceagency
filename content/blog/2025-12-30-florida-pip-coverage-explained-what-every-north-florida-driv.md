# Florida PIP Coverage Explained: What Every North Florida Driver Must Know in 2025

If you're driving in Florida, Personal Injury Protection (PIP) coverage isn't optional—it's the law. Personal Injury Protection is currently required for all Florida drivers. Florida operates as a no-fault state under Florida Statute §627.736, but major changes are coming that could transform how your auto insurance protects you and your family.

## Understanding Florida's No-Fault PIP System

PIP insurance Florida is a mandatory auto insurance coverage that protects you by paying 80% of medical expenses and 60% of lost wages up to $10,000, regardless of who caused an accident. This "no-fault" system means your own insurance company pays your initial medical bills and lost wages, even if another driver caused the crash.

### Current PIP Coverage Requirements

Every Florida driver must carry PIP coverage with these minimum benefits:

- **Medical Expenses**: PIP covers 80 percent of all necessary and reasonable medical expenses up to $10,000 resulting from a covered injury, no matter who caused the crash.
- **Lost Wages**: Covers 60% of your gross income lost due to accident-related injuries, up to the $10,000 policy limit.
- **Essential Services**: Pays for essential household services you cannot perform while injured, such as cleaning, cooking, or childcare.

### The Critical 14-Day Rule

Florida's 14-day rule requires you to get medical attention within two weeks of your accident, or you could lose your PIP benefits entirely. This isn't just a suggestion—it's a strict requirement that can void your entire claim if missed.

## The Emergency Medical Condition (EMC) Requirement

Here's where many Florida drivers get caught off guard: While the standard policy has a $10,000 minimum limit, you only get access to the full amount if a doctor certifies you have an Emergency Medical Condition (EMC). If your injuries don't qualify as an EMC, your benefits are capped at just $2,500.

This means that seemingly minor injuries could leave you with significantly reduced coverage. That's why seeking immediate medical attention—and ensuring proper documentation—is crucial after any accident.

## Major Changes Coming to Florida PIP in 2026

Florida's auto insurance landscape is about to undergo its biggest transformation in over 50 years. Starting July 1, 2026, Florida may eliminate its PIP requirement entirely. After more than 50 years of no-fault insurance, the state could end personal injury protection coverage and moving to a system that works differently for you and your family.

### What the New System Will Require

Under the new law, you would need to carry minimum bodily injury liability coverage of $25,000 per person and $50,000 per accident, plus $5,000 in medical payment (MedPay) coverage. This represents a complete shift in how your car insurance protects you after an accident.

The proposed changes include:
- Elimination of mandatory PIP coverage
- The bill also increases the minimum BI liability coverage limits from $10,000 per person and $20,000 per incident to $25,000 per person and $50,000 per incident, but the minimum property damage ("PD") liability coverage limit remains unchanged at $10,000.
- Required proof of bodily injury coverage at vehicle registration

## How PIP Works with Your Other Insurance

Understanding how PIP coordinates with your health insurance and other coverages is essential for North Florida drivers:

Your PIP acts as your primary coverage, paying out before your health insurance. Once PIP benefits are exhausted, your health insurance can act as secondary coverage.

### Coverage Scenarios

**If You're the Driver**: Your PIP coverage applies regardless of fault, providing immediate financial protection.

**As a Passenger**: PIP coverage follows you. If you're involved in an auto accident, as long as you come into contact with another motor vehicle, whether on a bicycle, as a pedestrian, in your car or someone else's vehicle, you're covered under your PIP policy

**Pedestrians and Cyclists**: Pedestrians and bicyclists are still covered under PIP insurance, even if their cars aren't involved in the accident. If an injured pedestrian or bicyclist owns a car, they're covered under their PIP auto policy.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Florida PIP

### What happens if I don't have PIP coverage?
Failure to maintain required insurance coverage in Florida may result in the suspension of your driver license/registration and a requirement to pay a reinstatement fee of up to $500.

### Can I increase my PIP limits above $10,000?
Yes, you can purchase additional PIP coverage or Medical Payments coverage to supplement the basic $10,000 minimum.

### Will my rates go up if I use my PIP coverage?
Your auto insurance rates will not increase if you were not at fault in the accident. Premiums are determined in Florida based upon this concept.

### How do I file a PIP claim?
Contact your insurance company immediately after an accident, seek medical attention within 14 days, and ensure all treatments are properly documented as medically necessary.

### What medical expenses does PIP not cover?
PIP doesn't cover the full 100% of medical expenses—only 80%. It also has specific requirements about what constitutes "necessary and reasonable" treatment.
