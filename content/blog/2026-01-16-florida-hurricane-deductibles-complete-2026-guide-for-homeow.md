# Florida Hurricane Deductibles: Complete 2026 Guide for Homeowners

As Florida homeowners prepare for another hurricane season, understanding your hurricane deductible has never been more critical. With Citizens Insurance planning to cut rates for 2026 by an average 2.6% statewide and significant market changes underway, many residents are reassessing their coverage options.

Your hurricane deductible isn't just another insurance term—it's a potentially significant out-of-pocket expense that could impact your family's financial recovery after a storm. Let's break down everything you need to know about Florida hurricane deductibles in 2026.

## What Is a Florida Hurricane Deductible?

If you have windstorm/hurricane coverage on your homeowners' policy, you may also have a separate hurricane deductible. This isn't your standard deductible—it's a separate amount you must pay before your insurance company begins covering hurricane-related damages.

To limit their exposure to catastrophic losses from natural disasters, insurers in these states sell homeowners insurance policies with percentage deductibles for storm damage instead of the traditional dollar deductibles, which are used for other types of losses such as fire damage and theft.

### How Hurricane Deductibles Differ from Standard Deductibles

Unlike your regular deductible (typically $500 to $2,000), percentage deductibles are based on the home's insured value. So if a house is insured for $300,000 and has a 5 percent deductible, the first $15,000 of a claim must be paid out of the policyholder's pocket.

When a hurricane deductible is applied, no other deductible under the policy may be applied, meaning you won't pay both deductibles for the same claim.

## Required Deductible Options in Florida

All insurers must offer a hurricane deductible of $500, 2 percent, 5 percent and 10 percent of the policy dwelling or structure limits. The percentages are based on the total value of the home.

Here's what these percentages mean in real dollars:

- **2% deductible on $300,000 home**: $6,000 out-of-pocket
- **5% deductible on $300,000 home**: $15,000 out-of-pocket  
- **10% deductible on $300,000 home**: $30,000 out-of-pocket

For North Florida homeowners, these amounts can represent significant financial exposure, making it essential to choose your deductible level carefully.

## When Does Your Hurricane Deductible Apply?

The duration of a hurricane in which the hurricane deductible would apply includes the time period beginning at the time a hurricane warning is issued for any part of Florida by the National Hurricane Center and ending 72 hours following the termination of the last hurricane watch or hurricane warning issued for any part of Florida by the National Hurricane Center.

### Key Trigger Points

A hurricane is defined in s. 627.4025, F.S., as a storm system that has been declared to be a "hurricane" by the National Hurricane Center of the National Weather Service. The deductible applies specifically when:

1. The National Hurricane Center declares a hurricane
2. A hurricane warning is issued for any part of Florida
3. Hurricane conditions exist anywhere in the state
4. Up to 72 hours after the last warning ends

If hurricane-force winds damaged your roof during a named storm event, you'll face the higher percentage-based deductible. However, if damage occurred from severe thunderstorms outside the hurricane deductible trigger period, your standard deductible applies.

## Types of Hurricane Deductibles

Some companies may have separate deductibles that will apply to either all windstorms, named storms only, or hurricanes:

### Windstorm Deductibles
When an insurance company offers a windstorm deductible, this applies to any windstorm regardless of whether it is a hurricane, tornado, or just an extra breezy day. Windstorm deductibles are the broadest of the three different deductible types.

### Named Storm Deductibles
Some carriers may have a "named storm" deductible instead. This means a separate deductible applies for windstorms that are named such as hurricanes and tropical storms, and your standard homeowner deductible would apply for any other wind loss.

### Hurricane-Only Deductibles
A hurricane deductible applies only to hurricanes. If a storm is downgraded from a hurricane to a tropical storm, for example, your hurricane deductible would not be invoked.

## Important Rules About Hurricane Deductible Application

### Once Per Season Rule
The hurricane deductible applies only once during a hurricane season. This means if you experience damage from multiple hurricanes in the same season, you'll only pay the hurricane deductible for the first qualifying event.

### Calendar Year vs. Hurricane Season
Some policies use a calendar year hurricane deductible instead of a per-storm deductible. Your policy has Coverage A of $280,000, a 2% hurricane deductible ($5,600), and a $1,000 AOP deductible. Storm 1 happens on July 20th, and you have expenses to remove a tree from the roof, tarp the roof, replace 15 shingles, and fix a portion of the fence. The total is $4,300; you're responsible for the $4,300... You're responsible for the remaining $1,300 due on the Calendar Year Hurricane Deductible.

## Strategies for Managing Hurricane Deductible Costs

### Building an Emergency Fund
Build an emergency fund to help cover the deductible in case of storm damage. Financial experts recommend setting aside funds equal to your hurricane deductible amount.

### Considering Lower Deductibles
Last year I noticed that my homeowner's insurance policy contained a 5% hurricane deductible, and I reached out to my insurance agent to determine what the increase in premium would be to lower the hurricane deductible to a 2% or 3% deductible. Surprisingly, the premium increase to lower the 5% hurricane deductible to 2% was less than $50 a year! Definitely worthwhile compared to the thousands saved if my home suffers a hurricane loss.

### Wind Mitigation Credits
Florida law requires insurers to offer discounts for hurricane-resistant features. Missing credits can cost hundreds per year. Inspection costs $75-$200. Get a wind mitigation inspection if you haven't, or if you've made improvements.

## What Your Hurricane Deductible Doesn't Cover

It's crucial to understand that no standard policy will offer coverage for flooding, which can also occur during hurricanes. A separate flood policy would need to be purchased.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How often can insurance companies apply my hurricane deductible?
The hurricane deductible applies only once during a hurricane season. However, the specific terms depend on whether your policy uses a per-storm or calendar year deductible structure.

### Can I choose a flat dollar hurricane deductible instead of a percentage?
Florida law allows you to choose between percentage-based and flat-dollar hurricane deductibles in some circumstances. All insurers must offer a $500 flat deductible option alongside percentage options.

### What happens if damage occurs outside the hurricane deductible trigger period?
If damage occurs before a hurricane warning is issued or more than 72 hours after warnings end, your standard all-other-perils deductible would typically apply instead of the hurricane deductible.

### Do I need both homeowners insurance with hurricane coverage and flood insurance?
Yes. Even homeowners insurance with a hurricane endorsement won't cover that flood damage. At the end of the day, it is often advisable to get comprehensive homeowners coverage that includes hurricane coverage and then procure supplemental flood insurance.

### How do I know if my hurricane deductible is being applied correctly?
Insurance companies sometimes misapply hurricane deductibles. We've seen carriers wrongly apply hurricane deductibles to damage that occurred before or after the official trigger period, or attribute pre-existing damage to a hurricane event to justify the higher deductible. This practice can cost Florida homeowners tens of thousands of dollars.
