## Understanding Florida's 15-Year Roof Rule

An insurer may not refuse to issue or refuse to renew a homeowner's policy insuring a residential structure with a roof that is less than 15 years old solely because of the age of the roof. For a roof that is at least 15 years old, an insurer must allow a homeowner to have a roof inspection performed by an authorized inspector at the homeowner's expense before requiring the replacement of the roof of a residential structure as a condition of issuing or renewing a homeowner's insurance policy.

This legislation, codified in Florida Statute 627.7011, provides important protections for homeowners:

### For Roofs Under 15 Years Old
If your roof is less than 15 years old, insurance companies cannot refuse to issue or renew your policy based solely on the roof's age. This protection gives homeowners peace of mind during the crucial early years of a roof's lifespan.

### For Roofs 15 Years or Older
The insurer may not refuse to issue or refuse to renew a homeowner's insurance policy solely because of roof age if an inspection of the roof of the residential structure performed by an authorized inspector indicates that the roof has 5 years or more of useful life remaining.

This means even older roofs can maintain insurance coverage if they pass professional inspection showing at least five years of remaining useful life.

## New 2026 Legislative Changes

The pending Senate Bill 808 introduces several important changes that will affect Florida homeowners:

### Expanded Inspector Qualifications
The term "authorized inspector" means an inspector who is approved by the insurer and who is: A home inspector licensed under s. 468.8314; A building code inspector certified under s. 468.607; A general, building, or residential contractor licensed under s. 489.111 or a roofing contractor; A professional engineer licensed under s. 471.015; A professional architect licensed under s. 481.213; Any other individual or entity recognized by the insurer as possessing the necessary qualifications to properly complete a general inspection of a residential structure insured with a homeowner's insurance policy.

### Roof Type Differentiation
The new law requires insurers to treat low-slope and steep-slope roofs differently when making coverage decisions, recognizing that different roof types have varying lifespans and maintenance requirements.

## Current Market Reality: Beyond the Legal Protections

While state law provides important protections, the practical reality in Florida's insurance market is more complex. Your insurance options will narrow significantly, and you can expect premiums to be 30-40% higher than for a home with a newer roof.

### Insurance Company Practices
Many insurers won't cover roofs over 20 years old. Some won't go beyond 15 years. Even with legal protections, insurance companies have developed various strategies to manage their exposure:

- Higher premiums for older roofs
- Limited coverage options
- Mandatory inspections at policy renewal
- Actual Cash Value (ACV) instead of Replacement Cost Value (RCV) coverage

### Citizens Property Insurance
Citizens Property considers a standard or architectural shingle roof old if it has been in place for 25 years or longer. The insurer considers slate, tile, clay, metal, or concrete roofs old if they have been in place for 50 years or longer.

As the state-backed insurer of last resort, Citizens has become Florida's largest home insurance provider due to private insurers leaving the market.

## Roof Materials and Insurance Implications

The type of roofing material significantly impacts your insurance options and rates:

### Asphalt Shingles
They scrutinize asphalt shingle roofs the most, becoming wary around the 15-year mark as the state's climate often shortens their lifespan. Most insurance issues arise with asphalt shingle roofs, which are most vulnerable to Florida's harsh climate.

### Metal Roofing
Metal roofs are highly favored due to their 40-70 year durability, making coverage much easier to secure. Insurance companies often give discounts for metal roofs.

### Tile Roofs
For tile roofs, the focus is not on the tiles themselves, which can last over 50 years, but on the underlayment beneath them. Its replacement, typically needed every 20-30 years, is what insurers consider a full "roof replacement."

## Florida Building Code Requirements

Your roof needs to withstand wind speeds up to 150 miles per hour in most areas. That's a serious requirement. Florida's building code mandates:

- **Wind Resistance**: Most Florida roofs must withstand minimum wind speeds of 150 mph.
- **Proper Fastening**: Florida requires decks to be fastened every 6 inches along the edges and every 12 inches in the field. The fasteners must be rated for your wind zone.
- **Approved Materials**: The state requires all roofing materials to meet specific wind and weather standards. If your roof doesn't meet these standards, you could face problems when it's time to file an insurance claim.

## Inspection Requirements and Process

### When Inspections Are Required
A roof certification inspection is a professional evaluation of your roof's age, condition, and remaining life expectancy. Insurance companies in Florida use this inspection to decide whether your home qualifies for or can renew an insurance policy.

### What Inspectors Look For
In order to pass the Roof Certification in Pinellas and Tampa, the roof must not leak, and must have at least 5 years of useful life remaining.

Professional inspectors evaluate:
- Overall roof condition and structural integrity
- Signs of leaks or water damage
- Proper ventilation systems
- Fastening and attachment methods
- Material condition and expected remaining lifespan

### Inspection Costs
You will also almost certainly need to pay for a professional roof inspection, which costs between $150 and $500, just to be considered for coverage.

## Proactive Steps for Homeowners

### Regular Maintenance
Regular roof maintenance, including inspections, repairs and cleaning, can extend the life of your roof and help you avoid issues during the 15-year inspection. Keeping detailed records of all maintenance and repairs can also be beneficial when dealing with insurance companies, providing evidence of your efforts to maintain the roof's condition.

### Documentation
Maintain comprehensive records including:
- Installation permits and certificates
- Contractor invoices and warranties
- Maintenance and repair receipts
- Previous inspection reports

### Pre-Hurricane Season Inspections
For Florida residents, the most vital time of year to have your roof inspected is the spring, before hurricane season kicks off and the rainy season begins so you can work to prevent storm damage.

## Understanding Coverage Types

### Replacement Cost vs. Actual Cash Value
Be cautious of "Actual Cash Value" (ACV) policies. They pay less on claims by factoring in your roof's age and wear (depreciation), leaving you with significant out-of-pocket costs for a replacement.

As your roof ages, many insurers switch from Replacement Cost Value (RCV) to Actual Cash Value (ACV) coverage, which can significantly impact your claim payouts.

If you receive a non-renewal notice:
1. Review the specific reason cited
2. Verify your roof's actual age
3. Consider getting a professional inspection
4. Contact your insurance agent immediately
5. Explore alternative carriers

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can my insurance company drop me if my roof is over 15 years old?

Florida law allows insurers to consider roof age when underwriting or renewing policies. Requirements vary by carrier, and inspections are commonly requested after 15 to 20 years. However, they cannot deny coverage solely based on age if your roof passes inspection showing five or more years of remaining useful life.

### What qualifies as an authorized inspector in Florida?

Authorized inspectors include licensed home inspectors, certified building code inspectors, licensed contractors, professional engineers, and professional architects. The new 2026 legislation expands these qualifications further.

### How is roof age calculated for insurance purposes?

For purposes of this subsection, a roof's age shall be calculated using the last date on which 100 percent of the roof's surface area was built or replaced in accordance with the building code in effect at that time or the initial date of a partial roof replacement when subsequent partial roof builds or replacements were completed that resulted in 100 percent of the roof's surface area being built or replaced.

### What happens if my roof fails inspection?

If your roof fails to show at least five years of remaining useful life, you may need to repair or replace it to maintain insurance coverage. Some insurers may offer limited coverage at higher premiums.

### Should I replace my roof before it reaches 15 years?

Not necessarily. We often see people rushing to replace perfectly good 12-year-old roofs because they're worried about insurance, when the law actually protects them. Focus on proper maintenance and be prepared for inspection when your roof reaches 15 years.
